Topic 5 Ethics– Revision

Ethical Issues

An ethical issue is a situation that raises questions about what is right and wrong.

Social and Cultural Issues

Social and cultural issues include the digital divide, privacy concerns related to surveillance, and the impact of social media.

Computing Laws Table

Law / Legal Area What it Protects Key Principles / Rules Example
Copyright Creative works such as text, music, images, and software code. Work cannot be copied, shared, or used without permission from the creator. Copying software or music without permission.
Data Protection Personal data collected, stored, and used by organisations. Data must be:
  • Collected lawfully and fairly
  • Used only for specific purposes
  • Accurate and up to date
  • Kept secure
  • Not kept longer than necessary
 A company must store customer data securely and only use it for the stated purpose.
Intellectual Property (IP) Creations of the human intellect such as inventions and creative works. Creators have legal rights over how their work is used and distributed. Protecting software or digital designs from illegal copying.
Patent Law Inventions and new processes. Gives the inventor exclusive rights to use, make, or sell the invention for up to 20 years. A new processor design protected so others cannot copy it for 20 years.
Trademark Law Brand names, logos, and slogans. Prevents other organisations from using the same or similar branding. Protecting a company logo from being used by another business.
Licensing How software and digital content can be used and distributed. Sets rules on how software can be installed, copied, or shared. A software licence that limits how many devices a program can be installed on.
Computer Misuse Law Computer systems and data from hacking and cybercrime. Makes unauthorised access, data theft, and system damage illegal. Laws that make unauthorised access to systems illegal.

Personal Data

Personal data is information that can be used to identify an individual. Examples include data collected using GPS, facial recognition, number plate recognition, smart devices, surveys, and official government or medical services.

Privacy Issues in Computing

Privacy issues include facial recognition, GPS tracking, and internet monitoring. These raise concerns about who is collecting data, how it is used, and who has access to it.

Internet Monitoring

Schools, businesses, and social media companies use monitoring software to track activity. This can help prevent harmful behaviour but raises concerns about free speech, misuse, and biased systems.

AI and Robotics Ethical Issues

Ethical concerns with AI and robotics include accountability, bias, safety, job displacement, and privacy.

Algorithmic Bias

Algorithmic bias can be caused by poor training data, human bias, or poor algorithm design. This can lead to discrimination and unfair outcomes.

Reducing Algorithmic Bias

Methods to reduce bias include human oversight, governance, and improving the quality of training data.

Environmental Impact of Computing

Computing impacts the environment through energy use, extraction of raw materials, data centres, electronic waste, and frequent replacement cycles.

Reducing Environmental Impact

Environmental impact can be reduced by keeping devices longer, repairing them, reusing or donating devices, and keeping software updated.

Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity is concerned with the protection of computer systems, computer networks, and data.

Its purpose is to protect computers and networks from cyberattacks, prevent unauthorised access, protect against damage caused by malicious software, prevent data theft, and protect against the disruption of services.

Cyber Security Threats

Cyber security threats are actions or weaknesses that can damage, disrupt, or take control of computer systems or data.

Malware Types

Type of Malware Description
Malware Software that has been purposely developed to damage, disrupt, or take control of computer systems.
Computer Virus Replicates itself and can transfer from one computer to another. Activated by a user, often through email attachments or when attached to other files or programs.
Trojan Disguises itself as legitimate software to gain access to a computer. Allows unauthorised backdoor access without the user being aware.
Spyware Records user activity such as keystrokes to capture passwords. Can also be used to control webcams and microphones.
Adware Automatically installs banners and pop-ups. Although it may not cause direct damage, it is undesirable and can slow down system performance.
Worm Spreads automatically across networks without human intervention by attaching itself to network tools.

Social Engineering

Social engineering techniques manipulate people into giving away confidential or personal information.

Social Engineering Techniques

Technique Description
Phishing An email or text message scam that tricks users into believing they are being contacted by a trusted organisation and encourages them to reveal sensitive information.
Pharming Redirects users to a fraudulent website that appears genuine, encouraging them to enter sensitive information.
Blagging (Pretexting) Fraudsters create a believable scenario to trick victims into revealing information they would not normally share.
Shoulder Surfing Attackers observe users entering passwords or PINs, often at public computers or ATMs.

Brute Force Attacks

Brute force attacks attempt to gain access by trying every possible combination of passwords.

Weak Passwords

Weak passwords are easy to guess. Dictionary attacks use algorithms to systematically try common words until the password is cracked.

Default Passwords

Default passwords are given to users when accounts are created. If not changed, they pose a major security risk, especially when sent unencrypted via email.

Removable Media

Removable media such as USB pen drives can transmit malware between computer systems.

Unpatched or Outdated Software

Software that is not regularly updated remains vulnerable to attack because security vulnerabilities have not been fixed.

Misconfigured Access Rights

Users should only have access to files and data they need. Incorrect access rights can give users access they should not have.

Penetration Testing

Penetration testing is legitimate testing of an organisation’s computer systems to identify vulnerabilities so they can be fixed before an attacker exploits them.

Preventing Cybersecurity Threats

Cybersecurity threats can be reduced using biometric measures, automatic software updates, secure password systems, email verification, and anti-virus software.

Methods of Preventing Cybersecurity Threats

Security Method Description
Biometric Security Uses fingerprints, facial recognition, or iris scans to verify a user’s identity. Biometrics are more secure than passwords because they cannot be guessed or forgotten.
Automatic Software Updates Keeps operating systems, firewalls, and antivirus software up to date to protect against new malware and recently discovered vulnerabilities.
Password Systems and Two-Factor Authentication Enforces strong passwords that must be changed regularly. Two-factor authentication requires an additional check such as a second password or confirmation via phone.
Email Verification Confirms a user’s identity by requiring them to activate a link sent to their email address.
Anti-Virus Software Scans files against a database of known virus signatures. Detected malware is quarantined. Antivirus software must be regularly updated to detect new threats.
