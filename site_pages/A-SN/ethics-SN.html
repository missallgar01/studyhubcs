Computing Related Legislation

The Data Protection Act (1998) / GDPR

Data protection law governs how personal data about living individuals is collected, stored, processed, and shared.

Personal data is any data that can identify a person directly or indirectly.

  • Name, address, email address
  • IP address combined with other data
  • Medical or employment records
Example:
A school storing student names, grades, and medical conditions must ensure the data is accurate, kept secure, and not shared without permission.

The Eight Data Protection Principles

  • Data must be processed fairly and lawfully
  • Used only for specific, legitimate purposes
  • Adequate, relevant, and not excessive
  • Accurate and kept up to date
  • Not kept longer than necessary
  • Processed in line with data subject rights
  • Protected against unauthorised access or loss
  • Not transferred outside protected regions without safeguards
Example:
A company collecting email addresses for password recovery must not later use them for marketing unless consent is given.

Computer Misuse Act (1990)

The Computer Misuse Act makes unauthorised access or modification of computer systems illegal.

  • Unauthorised access
  • Access with intent to commit further offences
  • Unauthorised modification of data
Examples:
  • Guessing a teacher’s password → offence 1
  • Accessing marks to change grades → offence 2 & 3
  • Installing malware → offence 3

Copyright, Design and Patents Act (1988)

This act protects original intellectual property such as music, films, logos, and software.

  • Copyright is automatic
  • Applies to computer programs
  • Lasts decades after creator’s death
Example:
Copying software from a friend or downloading pirated films is illegal under this act.

Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act (2000)

RIPA regulates surveillance and interception of communications by public authorities.

  • Allows monitoring with a legal warrant
  • ISPs must assist investigations
  • Used for national security and crime prevention
Example:
Police monitoring online communications of a terrorism suspect with court approval.

1.5.2 Moral & Ethical Issues

Moral and ethical issues arise when the use of computer systems affects individuals, society, or the environment. Designers and organisations must consider the impact of technology on people’s rights, wellbeing, and opportunities.

Computers in the Workforce

Computers and automation can replace human jobs but can also create new employment opportunities.

  • Robots replacing factory workers
  • Self-checkout machines reducing cashier jobs
  • AI chatbots replacing customer support staff
  • Automation increasing productivity but reducing employment
  • New technology jobs in software development and cybersecurity

Automated Decision Making

Automated decision-making systems use algorithms to make decisions without human intervention, which can lead to bias or unfair outcomes.

  • Algorithms deciding loan or mortgage approvals
  • Automated filtering of job applications
  • Facial recognition identifying suspects
  • Insurance premiums calculated automatically
  • Exam results calculated using algorithms

Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Artificial intelligence systems can perform tasks that normally require human intelligence, raising ethical concerns about control, responsibility, and bias.

  • AI diagnosing medical conditions
  • Voice assistants responding to user queries
  • Recommendation systems on streaming platforms
  • AI detecting fraud in banking systems
  • Self-driving vehicle decision-making systems

Environmental Effects of Computing

The production, use, and disposal of computer systems can have significant environmental impacts.

  • Electronic waste (e-waste) sent to landfill
  • High energy consumption of data centres
  • Carbon emissions from cryptocurrency mining
  • Short device lifecycles increasing waste
  • Mining of rare earth metals for devices

Monitoring Behaviour

Monitoring systems track user behaviour, raising concerns about privacy and personal freedom.

  • Employers monitoring employee activity
  • CCTV surveillance in public spaces
  • GPS tracking of delivery drivers
  • Schools monitoring student internet usage
  • Smart devices collecting usage data

Analysing Personal Information

Personal data can be analysed to predict behaviour, but this raises ethical concerns about consent and misuse.

  • Targeted advertising based on browsing history
  • Health apps predicting medical risks
  • Social media profiling users
  • Political advertising targeting voters
  • Retailers analysing shopping habits

Piracy and Offensive Communications

The misuse of technology to share illegal or harmful content raises serious ethical and legal concerns.

  • Illegal streaming of films or live sports
  • Downloading pirated software or music
  • Sending abusive or threatening messages online
  • Cyberbullying on social media platforms
  • Sharing copyrighted textbooks without permission

Layout, Colour & Character Sets

User interface design must consider accessibility, usability, and cultural differences to ensure systems are inclusive.

Accessibility Issues

  • High-contrast colours for visually impaired users
  • Clear fonts for users with dyslexia
  • Avoiding red/green colour combinations
  • Icons used alongside text
  • Scalable text for different screen sizes

Cultural Issues

  • Reading direction differences (left-to-right vs right-to-left languages)
  • Colour meanings vary between cultures
  • Icons and symbols may be offensive or confusing in some cultures
  • Character sets must support non-Latin alphabets using Unicode
  • Date, number, and currency formats differ internationally
